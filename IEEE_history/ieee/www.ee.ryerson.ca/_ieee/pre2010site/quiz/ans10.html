THE IEEE ELECTRICAL ENGINEERING ONLINE QUIZ

QUESTION#10: OIL FILLED CAPACITORS
Difficulty: not-rated

ANSWER IS:

1. Charge is conserved. Q = CV, so the voltage goes to 100 volts.

2. Extra credit: Energy is 1/2 CV 2 . The energy in the capacitor goes from 1/2 millijoule to 5 millijoules. The extra energy is supplied by whatever force removed the oil from between the plates – in this case, by gravity, which had to pull the oil away, working against the electric field which was holding the oil.

3. More extra credit: If the energy stored in the capacitor is high or the experiment were performed in low gravity, the oil could be held between the plates. The capacitance would remain the same and so would the energy stored in the capacitor.

EE-Quiz reader Michelle Youn adds some detail: C = epsilon (A/d), where epsilon is the dielectric constant, A is the surface area of the plates, and d is the distance between the plates. A and d remain the same and if the oil stays in place, epsilon does not change, so the capacitance and stored energy remain the same.

There is a more well known manifestation of this effect. After sitting in your car for a while, rubbing against the seat, you may, due to triboelectricity, charge up to some voltage relative to the car (triboelectricity is buildup of a static electric charge due to friction). When you stop and get out, you pull apart the plates of the capacitor (you are one plate, the car is the other). The capacitance drops, the voltage goes roaring up and a half inch arc strikes from your fingertip to the door handle. Ouch.
